Paycrave

iOS mobile app

Summary

Paycrave is a mobile app designed to allow food truck fans to locate, review and purchase menu items from nearby food trucks. I started this project with extensive research followed by an iterative process of designing and redesigning based on usability testing.

Checking out the Competition

The first step was to find out what mobile apps already exist and are successful in the foodspace. I looked at direct competition (other food truck apps) as well as individual features from other apps related to food.

what are food apps mainly used for?

  1. Locating places to eat.
  2. Discovering places to eat.
  3. Ordering food for delivery or pick up.

observations and trends: reading user reviews, checking updates

  1. Users prefer simpler apps that perform fewer tasks to those that perform many but focus on none.
  2. Users prefer scrolling over clicking through multiple pages.
  3. Users want to keep a record of past activity
  4. On a practical level, until it becomes popular amongst a large vendor base, the app's functionality and aesthetic should not rely too heavily on pictures.

what does this mean for me?

Paycrave should be designed around fulfilling only one or two main uses. Other tasks should be designed as if they were convenient additional features. The aesthetic layout of the app should be simple, clear and unambiguously function-centered. Paycrave should also be designed to cultivate a continuing relationship with the user; it should retain information that makes each progressive use of the app easier and more enjoyable for the user.

Understanding the User: User Surveys

The next step was to figure out who was likely to use a mobile app for food trucks, and what they would want to be able to use it for. I sent out a survey to collect this information.

Analyzing Results and Forming a Vision

who would use Paycrave?

The survey indicated that the most likely user of a mobile food-truck app were professionals between the ages of 25 - 50 who worked in a city and walk 15 minutes to grab lunch or dinner a couple times a week.

how would they use Paycrave?

The survey indicated that users would more commonly use a food-truck app for on-the-spot decision making than for planning for future meals even though the majority of people would use the app for both tasks at one point or another. Ability to discover and locate places to eat were valued significantly higher than the ability to order food. Quality of food, by far, was the most important determining factor when deciding where to eat.

translating data into a product

My results tell me that users want an app that would allow them to locate nearby trucks and show them enough information (ratings, food type) upon first glance to choose trucks that sound appealing. One more click will show them reviews, a highlighted feature, and general information, and another click away shows them the menu list with prices. As a secondary feature, another couple clicks would allow the user to purchase food for pickup. While Paycrave allows the user to perform a multitude of other tasks and keeps a record of all activity, these capabilities are very slightly more embedded and do not obstruct the user flow. Paycrave is split into two sides. Its default side allows users to locate and discover nearby food trucks. The second half of the app is an editable record of all the user's account information and past activities.

Planning and Execution

user stories

As a user...

  • I want to view food trucks near me on a map.
  • I want to view food trucks near me in a list.
  • I want to select a truck, view details and see a menu.
  • I want to be able to search for my favorite truck.
  • I want to create an account and view recent transactions.
  • I want to choose items and pay for them using my phone.
  • I want to rate and review a food truck and view other ratings and reviews.
  • I want to mark my favorite food trucks.

creating wireframes

After determining the layout and flow of Paycrave, I began making some low-fidelity wireframes to map out the layout and behavior of each page.

Testing Usability with High- Fidelity Wireframes

I wireframed each page and demonstrated their behavior by linking them in InVision. I then observed as users clicked through the linked wireframes and sent out another survey asking participants for feedback as they performed the different user stories.

taking user feedback into consideration

After observing users click through Paycrave and receiving feedback from my usability survey, I further refined my wireframes. One main change I made was to modify each page to make obvious which pages scroll and which pages do not scroll. Check out the final version of my clickable wireframes in inVision below.
