Background

Found Eats is a start-up ecommerce for specialty foods where small food makers can sell their products online to customers seeking artisanal foods. Food makers are directed to the vendor-facing side of the site where they must complete the set-up process to become an active seller on Found Eats. After the initial set-up is complete, vendor accounts will be used to manage orders, update products details and manage shipping and payment. This project is a redesign of the current Found Eats Vendor Account website.

user research

Use Cases

  1. I am a new vendor who is going through the set-up process the first time.
  2. I am a vendor who is already up and live on the site, and am visiting the site to modify my information or products.
  3. I am a live vendor checking my account to maintain the orders that come in through the site.

My redesign centers around fixing the problems created by Use Case #1. Ultimately the redesign creates more separation between the different use cases, clarifying both the set-up process and site management, but because Found Eats is in its early stages, successfully signing on new vendors is the main priority.

Understanding the User

The first step to designing a user-friendly site is to get to know the user. Significant amounts of time have been dedicated to not only walking vendors through the set-up process, allowing me to observe how they interact with the website, but also talking talking to the vendors about their hopes for their companies and what role they see Found Eats playing in achieving their goals. Through this process, I was able connect with my users' personal stories and understand their daily routines. I was soon able to identify how subtle differences in communication distinguished between the vendors feeling comforted and reassured, versus skeptical or intimidated. From there, I began brainstorming underlying reasons for the problems we were seeing with the current website.

ideation and strategizing

Problems with the Current Site

  1. Vendors are unclear what sections they are required to complete in order to finish the sign up process.
  2. Vendors are unclear what information goes into the input fields.
  3. Vendors are becoming sloppy with their product and company descriptions as they rush through it due to how long and involved it seems.
  4. Vendors are not completing the sign up process due to frustration with the process and waning interest.

These problems should all be considered in the context that Found Eats is new to the food space, and the website has only recently launched. Because it is still in it's early stages, the website itself has not been able to fully embody the look, feel and functionality that its idea was founded on. A large portion of vendors are therefore already skeptical about committing time and effort into Found Eats, and generally speaking prioritize cultivating other relationships with more established companies higher than this one. There is little to no true precedent for the ideal style and structure for the vendors to follow when setting up their company accounts.

My Goals

creating a short, direct path to completion

My efforts were directed firstly at creating a clear trajectory of actions that a vendor must take before their account is complete. The pop-up walkthrough behaves like a tour guide that directs the user to each subsequent step of the set-up process. The presence of a virtual authority diffuses the illusion of the users' decision-making responsibilities. This portrays a confident company image allowing the user to trust the error validation system, minimizing opportunities to linger, second-guess and re-interpret what each question is asking for. My goal is to create a short, clear path for the user. By combining thematically similar sections, and replacing click away pages with accordian tabs that can be edited on the same page, the perceived number of steps to complete is greatly reduced. This reduced number of steps is reflected on the progress bar that appears on every page. The overall effect should make the user feel more oriented in their location of the site and confident in an shorter, easier set-up process.


building excitement

The second major priority was to create a sense of excitement as the user progresses through the set-up process. The global progress bar and local checklist mark each completed page and entry-field as an accomplishment and additional step towards the finish. However, mimicking the customer-facing marketplace more than anything, should ideally ellicit an emotional response from the user. As the vendors work through each entry field and upload each picture, they can see its significance in how they are cultivating their company's image on the live site; By having in-place click-to-edit elements, they can see exactly how they are telling their story. Completing the vendor account no longer seems like a necessary and daunting chore, but instead a means by which they can communicate with the foodie community.


reducing errors

My last main priority was to reduce the number of errors that were occuring from vendors filling in incorrect information. Using the fixed right panel underneath the local checklist, as a place for FAQ's, and utilizing in place hover explanations on each given element should accomplish this. Mimicking the live marketplace, and using answer validation should additionally help solve this problem.


side-note: business objectives

The broad business objectives of my redesign are to increase profits and to reduce cost. This is achieved by reducing the number of problems occurring from the current design, further automating the vendor sign-up process and using the new website as an opportunity to implement a new shipping policy. The resulting product requires minimal maintenance allowing the company to handle a much larger influx of vendors, meanwhile reducing the staff budget. On the customer- facing marketplace, the changes to the vendor account website will increase the conversion rate of customers browsing through products to customers actively buying products on Found Eats, and will increase profits from individual sales.
